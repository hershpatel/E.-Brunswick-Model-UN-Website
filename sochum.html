    The Social, Humanitarian, and Cultural Committee of the United Nations has always focused on the examination of human rights. Currently in its 96th session, SOCHUM (as it’s commonly referred to as) deals with issues spanning from drug trafficking to indigenous people, which is the focus of this session. Indigenous Peoples worldwide number between 300-500 million, embody and nurture 80% of the world’s cultural and biological diversity, and occupy 20% of the world’s land surface. However, their lands have often been taken from them due to discriminatory government policies, and actions of private economic interests. They are often in the center of conflict for access to valuable resources because of where they live, or, in yet other cases, struggling to live the way they would like. There is not yet one definitive definition of Indigenous Peoples, but they are generally considered as those who inhabited a land before it was conquered by colonial societies and who consider themselves distinct from the societies currently governing those territories.

    The UN Declaration on the Rights of Indigenous Peoples is a major achievement by Indigenous peoples in the face of state reluctance, and sometimes, active resistance, to address the issues contained in it. However, there is still much controversy surrounding it. The purpose of this committee is to create a potentially revised resolution, determining the rights of Indigenous Peoples and what the United Nations should do in ensuring that these individuals are given the rights that they are entitled to.
